MFA vs passwordless: Loading friction where it matters.

Category: Access • 6 min read

Not all multi-factor journeys end in a push notification. Let’s demystify where MFA is still the guard, and where passwordless flips the switch.

MFA pads every login with a second factor, usually a push, OTP, or hardware key. It answers: “Is this who they say they are?” passwordless, on the other hand, replaces passwords entirely with device credentials, biometrics, or cryptographic assertions.

The best deployments combine both. For day-to-day, passwordless removes the friction of typing credentials (Windows Hello, FIDO2). When risk spikes—a new location, flagged device, or high-value app—the system demands a second factor.

The flow is: user requests access → identity provider checks device posture + risk score → either allows passwordless handshake or ejects to MFA challenge. If you run the risk engine yourself, keep risk thresholds and fallback logic documented.

Hardware keys still matter. They are the gold standard for phishing resistance, and they bootstrap passwordless. The user proves possession, and the key signs the request. No shared secret ever leaves their pocket.

Rollout guidance: pilot passwordless with low-risk internal apps, collect error rates, and then gate external SaaS with conditional MFA. Document the fallback—what happens when a user loses their key? The answer should be a manual verification channel.

Takeaway: Passwordless removes the guessable secret; MFA adds layered trust when risk surfaces. Keep both in your stack and orchestrate the transition with risk-aware logic.
